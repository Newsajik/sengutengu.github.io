Chrysomallon squamiferum:

The scaly-foot snail

Final project for Deep Sea Biology (BIOL 4030), Prof. Heather Olins, Summer 2024, Boston College

Introduction

Chrysomallon squamiferum or the scaly-foot snail is a gastropod found at hydrothermal vents on the ocean floor. Scroll down to look at some of its amazing anatomical features.

Perhaps its most distinctive feature are the armor-like scales, or sclerites, covering its footâ€”they are composed of iron and sulfur, making the scaly-foot snail the only known organism to use sulfur (instead of oxygen) for biomineralization (Chen et al. 2017).
The shell of C. squamiferum is also composed of iron and sulfur, with a core of aragonite within (source).
The nervous system of C. squamiferum is small, suggesting that it does not need high intelligence to survive.
However, C. squamiferum has a very large gill and heart to supply nutrients to the symbiotic bacteria living in its esophageal gland.

The story with C. squamiferum

The Indian Ocean has a bunch of hydrothermal vents.
Scaly-foot snails were first found in the Kairei hydrothermal vent field in Southwestern Indian Ocean by Van Dover et al. in 2001, using ROV Jason. The snail was subsequently characterized by Waren et al. 2003, and named by Chen et al. much later, in 2015.
In 2009, two other populations of scaly-foot snails were found in Solitaire (Nakamura et al.) and Longqi (Tao et al. 2012) hydrothermal vent fields.
while all three populations were determined to belong to one species (cite), the Longqi population was much more genetically distant from the Kairei and Solitaire populations. This suggests that larvae do not tend to travel very far.
