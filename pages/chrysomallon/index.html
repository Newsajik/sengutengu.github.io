Anatomy

Scroll down to look at some of its amazing anatomical features.

This is a scaly-foot snail from Nakamura et al. 2012. Though it's only about 2 inches in length, it's gigantic for snails in its family, Peltospiridae. The increased size may be an adaptation to increase the amount of symbiotic bacteria it can host.

Perhaps the most distinctive feature of C. squamiferum is the armor-like scales, or sclerites, covering its foot—they are composed of iron and sulfur, making the scaly-foot snail the only known organism to use sulfur (instead of oxygen) for biomineralization (Chen et al. 2017).

The shell of C. squamiferum is also composed of iron and sulfur, with a core of aragonite within (source).

The nervous system of C. squamiferum is small, suggesting that it does not need high intelligence to survive.

However, C. squamiferum has a very large gill (ctenidium) and heart to supply nutrients to the symbiotic bacteria living in its esophageal gland.

Discovering C. squamiferum

The Indian Ocean is criss-crossed with four mid-ocean ridges—the Carlsberg Ridge, Central Indian Ridge, the South West Indian Ridge, and the South East Indian Ridge—where magma rises and hardens into basalt, forming new oceanic crust. These ridges are dotted along their lengths with active hydrothermal vents.

Scaly-foot snails were first found in the Kairei hydrothermal vent field in Southwestern Indian Ocean by Van Dover et al. in 2001, using ROV Jason. The snail was subsequently characterized by Waren et al. 2003, and named by Chen et al. much later, in 2015.

In 2009, two other populations of scaly-foot snails were found in Solitaire (Nakamura et al.) and Longqi (Tao et al. 2012) hydrothermal vent fields.

While all three populations were determined to belong to one species (cite), the Longqi population (blue) was much more genetically distant from the Kairei and Solitaire populations (red). This suggests that larvae do not tend to travel very far.

Endosymbiosis

C. squamiferum appears not to eat. Instead, it hosts endosymbiotic bacteria that produces all of its energy and food.

We, above the surface, eat plants or eat organisms that have eaten plants. Plants are an example of photoautotrophs. Photoautotrophs derive energy from sunlight to fix inorganic carbon into organic matter (e.g. glucose).

On the ocean floor, there is no sunlight. So how do organisms here eat? There is a trickle of organic matter from the surface called marine snow, but it is not enough for C. squamiferum. Instead, C. squamiferum uses gamma-proteobacteria that can harness the chemical energy of deep sea hydrothermal vents to fix inorganic carbon into organic matter.

The bacteria in C. squamiferum are able to provide many of the missing synthetic pathways in C. squamiferum, including all 20 amino acids.
